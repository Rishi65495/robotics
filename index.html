1. Classification of Robots

Definitions (Know all three for exams)

  • British Definition: A reprogrammable device with multiple DOF that can manipulate and transport parts for manufacturing.
  • ISO Definition: An automatic, servo-controlled, freely programmable, multipurpose manipulator for handling work pieces, tools, or special devices.
  • RIA (American) Definition: A reprogrammable, multifunctional manipulator designed to move material through variable programmed motions.
Analogy: Think of a robot like an advanced washing machine. A normal washing machine runs a fixed program. A robot is like one that can be completely reprogrammed — today it washes clothes, tomorrow it can be taught to mix paint — and it can physically reach out to handle objects.

Asimov's Laws of Robotics (Common Exam Question)

  1. A robot may not injure a human or allow one to come to harm through inaction.
  2. A robot must obey orders given by humans, except where they conflict with Law 1.
  3. A robot must protect its own existence, except where this conflicts with Laws 1 or 2.
  4. Zeroth Law (added later): A robot may not harm humanity as a whole, or allow it to come to harm — overrides all others.
Exam Tip: These laws were introduced in Asimov's 1942 short story "Runaround." The Zeroth Law was added later as a higher priority law. Remember: the zeroth law protects humanity as a whole, not just individuals.

2. Robot Manipulator Kinematics

Basic Definitions

  • Links: Rigid members of a robot that form its structure. Rigid = distance between any two points on a link never changes. Think of bones in your arm.
  • Joints: Movable connections between links (also called axes). Like your elbow or knee.
  • Actuators: Motors/devices (electric, hydraulic, pneumatic) that apply force/torque to joints to cause movement.
  • End-Effectors: The "hand" at the end of the robot arm. Can be a gripper, welding gun, spray painter, etc.
Analogy: Think of the robot arm as your arm. Links = bones (humerus, radius). Joints = shoulder, elbow, wrist. Actuator = your muscles. End-effector = your hand/tool.

Configuration Space (C-Space)

  • Configuration: The position of all links/points of a rigid body. Needs minimum number of real-valued coordinates.
  • DOF (Degrees of Freedom): Minimum number of coordinates needed to fully describe a body's position.
  • In 2D space: DOF = 3 (x translation, y translation, 1 rotation about z-axis)
  • In 3D space: DOF = 6 (x, y, z translations + rotation about x, y, z axes)
DOF = (Dimension of C-Space) − (Number of Independent Holonomic Constraints)

Types of Constraints

TypeDefinitionExample
HolonomicExpressible algebraically in coordinates. Restrict motion to lower-dimensional subspace.Fixed joints, hinges, sliding connections
Non-HolonomicCannot be expressed algebraically; restrict velocities/accelerations, not just positions.Rolling without slipping (car wheel), friction interactions
Analogy for Non-Holonomic: A car can only go forward/backward in the direction it's facing. It can't slide sideways instantly. The constraint is on the direction of velocity — that's non-holonomic.

Constraints: Independent vs. Redundant

  • Independent constraints: Each one actually restricts new motion. Count these.
  • Redundant constraints: Already blocked by another constraint. Do NOT count these.

Key Memory Trick: Higher DOF joint = fewer constraints imposed. Revolute/Prismatic = most constraints (5 spatial). Spherical = fewest constraints (3 spatial). Cylindrical and Universal sit in between (4 spatial).

Industrial Robot Joint Types (5 Types Used in Practice)

  1. Linear joint (L): Translational sliding, links parallel to each other.
  2. Orthogonal joint (U): Translational sliding, links perpendicular to each other.
  3. Rotational joint (R): Rotation, axis perpendicular to both link axes.
  4. Twisting joint (T): Rotation, axis parallel to both link axes.
  5. Revolving joint (V): Input link axis parallel to rotation axis; output link perpendicular to rotation axis.

Robot Anatomy — Joint-Link Numbering

  • Base + its connection to first joint = Link 0
  • First joint = Joint 1; its output = Link 1
  • Link 1 is simultaneously the output of Joint 1 and the input of Joint 2.
  • This chain continues to the end effector.
Analogy: Like floors in a building. Ground floor (Link 0) → Door 1 (Joint 1) → Room 1 (Link 1) → Door 2 (Joint 2) → Room 2 (Link 2), and so on.

4. Arm Control & Wrist Control

Body-and-Arm Configurations (4 Main Types)

A) Polar / Spherical Configuration (RRP)

  • Joint notation: TRL (Twisting, Rotating, Linear)
  • Structure: Rotating base + revolute joint + prismatic (extending) arm
  • Work volume: Partial sphere
  • Advantage: Long horizontal reach; can reach floor-level objects
  • Disadvantage: Low vertical reach, complex construction, accuracy drops with radial extension
  • Examples: Unimate 2000, MAKER 110; used in machining, spray painting

B) Cylindrical Configuration (RPP)

  • Joint notation: TLO (Twisting, Linear, Orthogonal)
  • Structure: Rotating column + vertical slide + horizontal extend arm
  • Work volume: Approximates a cylinder
  • Advantage: Good mechanical stiffness, increased rigidity, high payload
  • Disadvantage: Less work volume, accuracy drops as horizontal stroke increases
  • Example: GMF model M-1A; used in machine loading, narrow horizontal access

C) Cartesian / Rectangular Configuration (PPP)

  • Joint notation: LOO (Linear, Orthogonal, Orthogonal)
  • Structure: Three perpendicular prismatic (linear) joints
  • Work volume: Cuboid (box-shaped)
  • Advantage: Highest precision, simple programming, lower cost, high payloads
  • Disadvantage: Limited flexibility, reduced work envelope, low dexterity
  • Example: IBM RS-1; used to lift/move heavy loads

D) Jointed Arm / Articulated Configuration (RRR)

  • Joint notation: TRR (Twisting, Rotating, Rotating)
  • Structure: Mimics human arm — waist (T), shoulder (R), elbow (R)
  • Work volume: Spherical — largest work envelope
  • Advantage: Maximum flexibility, huge work volume, quick operation
  • Disadvantage: Very expensive, complex operating procedures, many components
  • Example: SCARA robot; used for arc welding, spray coating
Analogy for RRR: If Cartesian robots are like elevators (strictly linear), articulated robots are like a human arm — they can reach around corners, under tables, and behind objects with ease.

SCARA Robot (Special Case)

  • Full Name: Selective Compliance Assembly Robot Arm
  • Shoulder and elbow axes are vertical (unlike standard jointed arm)
  • Very rigid vertically, but compliant (flexible) horizontally
  • Ideal for insertion-type assembly — no separate wrist needed

Robot Wrist — Three DOF

  • Notation: RRT
  • Roll (R): Rotation of wrist about the arm axis
  • Pitch (P): Up or down rotation (like nodding head)
  • Yaw (Y): Right or left rotation (like shaking head "no")
Analogy: The wrist in a robot is exactly like your wrist — it orients the hand/tool in space. The arm+body (shoulder/elbow) positions where you reach; the wrist decides which way the hand faces.

Six DOF Robot

A full 6-DOF robot = 3 body/arm movements + 3 wrist movements. This allows the end effector to reach any position in its work volume with any orientation — mimicking the full range of the human arm.

Precision of Movement — Three Metrics

TermDefinitionKey Formula / Note
Spatial Resolution Smallest increment of motion at wrist that can be controlled Control Resolution = Range / Control Increments
Accuracy Ability to reach a specified target point = ½ distance between two adjacent resolution points. Hard to control — manufacturers rarely specify it.
Repeatability Ability to return to a previously visited point consistently Repeatability errors are random. Larger robots have less precise repeatability. Non-servo robots can achieve ±0.01 inch.
Key Relationship: Resolution → Accuracy → Repeatability. All three are affected by mechanical inaccuracies. Repeatability is generally better than accuracy for most industrial robots.

End Effectors — Two Types

  1. Grippers: Grasp and manipulate objects
    • Mechanical grippers (2-finger, 3-finger)
    • Suction/vacuum cups
    • Magnetized grippers
    • Multi-fingered grippers (human-hand-like)
  2. Tools: Process-specific tools attached to wrist
    • Spot welding gun, arc welding tools
    • Spray painting gun
    • Drilling spindle, grinders, wire brushes
    • Hooks, scoops (for fluids), heating torches

Coordinate Systems

Tool Coordinate SystemBody FrameAxis system defined relative to orientation of wrist faceplate (where end effector attaches). Moves relative to tool orientation.

Types of Robot Control Systems (4 Types)

  1. Limited Sequence Control: Simplest. Uses mechanical stops at end positions. Pick-and-place only. No feedback between endpoints.
  2. Playback with Point-to-Point Control: Records sequence of discrete points, then replays. Controller computes path between points.
  3. Playback with Continuous Path Control: Records smooth path through space. Greater memory needed. Used for spray painting, welding.
  4. Intelligent Control: Responds to sensor inputs, makes decisions, communicates with humans. Highest level.

Motion Control — Two Key Aspects

  • Path Control: How accurately the robot traces a given path. Critical for gluing, painting, welding.
  • Velocity Control: How well velocity is maintained. Critical for gluing and painting to ensure uniform coating thickness and stable process parameters.

Point-to-Point Control (PTP)

  • Only end points are programmed; path between them computed by controller
  • User can control velocity; may permit linear or piecewise linear motion
  • Feedback control verifies each joint reaches desired location
  • Loads up to 500 lb
  • Applications: Pick-and-place, palletizing, machine loading, spray painting, arc welding

Continuous Path Control (CP)

  • Robot can stop at any point along the controlled path
  • All path points must be stored in robot's memory
  • Programmer manually moves the arm through desired path — controller records many individual points (teach-in method)
  • Applications: Straight-line motion, spray painting, finishing, gluing, arc welding

Controlled-Path Control

  • Control equipment can generate paths of different geometry: straight lines, circles, interpolated curves — with high accuracy
  • Only start/finish points + path definition function stored in memory
  • All controlled-path robots have servo capability to correct their path
  • Applications: Precision welding, high-accuracy spray painting, complex gluing, contour finishing

6. Open Loop vs. Closed Loop Control

Open Loop Control

  • Output does NOT affect control action
  • No feedback mechanism
  • Simple, cheap, fast
  • Low accuracy, less reliable
  • Low maintenance
  • Example: Clothes dryer with a timer — stops after set time whether clothes are dry or not

Closed Loop Control

  • Output feeds back and influences control action
  • Generates error signal = (desired output − actual output)
  • Accurate, reliable, expensive
  • Requires high maintenance
  • Example: Air conditioner — thermostat compares room temp to set temp and adjusts compressor

Non-Servo vs. Servo Control

Analogy for Non-Servo: Like a light switch — only ON or OFF, no dimming. Like a servo: like a dimmer switch — you can precisely set any brightness level and it holds there.

8. Translational & Rotational Motion

Translational Motion

  • Also called linear motion — movement along a straight line
  • In a robot: represented by prismatic joints moving along X, Y, or Z axes
  • Described by linear displacement, linear velocity, and linear acceleration
  • Example: A gripper moving straight down to pick up a part from a conveyor

Rotational Motion

  • Movement about an axis — rotation or angular motion
  • In a robot: represented by revolute joints rotating about X, Y, or Z axes
  • Described by angular displacement (θ), angular velocity (ω), and angular acceleration (α)
  • Example: A robot's waist turning (twisting joint T) to face a different direction

Combined Motion in Robots

  • Most robot movements combine translation and rotation simultaneously
  • The helical joint is a perfect example: rotation + translation linked together (screw-like motion)
  • Cylindrical joints allow independent rotation and translation about the same axis
Analogy: Translational motion is like a train moving on tracks (straight line). Rotational motion is like a spinning wheel. A cylindrical joint is like an office chair: you can spin it (rotation) AND raise/lower it (translation) independently.

Motion Conversion

  • Robots often need to convert rotational actuator output into linear motion (or vice versa)
  • Methods:
    • Rack and Pinion: Rotary gear (pinion) meshes with linear rack → converts rotation to translation
    • Lead Screw / Ball Screw: Rotating screw → linear nut movement (like a car jack)
    • Crank-Slider Mechanism: Converts rotation to reciprocating linear motion (like a piston in an engine)
    • Hydraulic/Pneumatic Cylinders: Fluid pressure → linear push/pull force
Example: A ball screw is commonly used in CNC machines and robot joints. When the motor (rotational) spins the screw, the nut on the screw moves linearly along the axis — converting rotation to precise linear motion with minimal backlash.

9. Lagrangian Analysis of Manipulators

What is Lagrangian Mechanics?

The Lagrangian approach is a powerful method to derive the equations of motion for a mechanical system. Instead of analyzing all forces (Newton), it works with energy.

L = T − V Where: L = Lagrangian, T = Total Kinetic Energy, V = Total Potential Energy
Analogy: Newton's method is like tracking every force on every part of a system (complicated for robots with many joints). Lagrangian is like using a GPS navigation system — you care only about your current state (speed, position) and energy, not every individual force. The equations still get you to the right answer, often more elegantly.

Why Lagrangian for Robots?

  • Robot manipulators have multiple links and joints — tracking all forces/torques with Newton's method becomes extremely complex.
  • Lagrangian naturally handles constraints (joint limits, fixed links) through generalized coordinates.
  • Works with joint variables as generalized coordinates (θ₁, θ₂, … for revolute joints).

Lagrangian Equations of Motion

d/dt (∂L/∂q̇ᵢ) − ∂L/∂qᵢ = τᵢ Where: qᵢ = generalized coordinate (joint variable), q̇ᵢ = its velocity, τᵢ = generalized force/torque applied at joint i

Steps for Lagrangian Analysis of a Robot Arm

  1. Choose generalized coordinates (joint angles θ₁, θ₂, ... for each joint)
  2. Calculate Kinetic Energy (T) of all links (translational + rotational components)
  3. Calculate Potential Energy (V) of all links (due to gravity acting on center of mass)
  4. Form the Lagrangian: L = T − V
  5. Apply Lagrange's equations for each joint to get equations of motion
  6. Solve for joint torques required to achieve desired motion
Example — 2-Link Robot Arm: For a 2-link planar robot arm with joint angles θ₁ and θ₂:
T = ½m₁(l₁θ̇₁)² + ½m₂[(l₁θ̇₁)² + (l₂θ̇₂)² + 2l₁l₂θ̇₁θ̇₂cos(θ₂)]
V = m₁gl₁sin(θ₁) + m₂g[l₁sin(θ₁) + l₂sin(θ₁+θ₂)]
Applying Lagrange's equation gives the torques needed at each joint.

Forward and Inverse Kinematics

TypeGivenFindDifficulty
Forward Kinematics Joint angles (θ₁, θ₂, …) Position & orientation of end effector Straightforward (one unique answer)
Inverse Kinematics Desired end-effector position & orientation Required joint angles Complex (multiple solutions may exist, or no solution)
Analogy: Forward Kinematics: "If I bend my elbow 90° and raise my shoulder 45°, where does my hand end up?" (Easy to calculate). Inverse Kinematics: "I want my hand at point (x, y, z). What angle should I set my elbow and shoulder?" (Complex — multiple body positions can reach the same point.)

10. Control of Actuators

What are Actuators?

Actuators are the "muscles" of a robot — devices that convert energy into mechanical motion (force or torque) to move the joints.

Types of Actuators

TypeEnergy SourceAdvantagesDisadvantagesApplications
Electric (DC/AC Motors) Electrical Clean, precise, easy to control; high repeatability Limited force/torque at low speeds; needs gearbox Most modern robots (SCARA, collaborative robots)
Stepper Motors Electrical Precise positioning without encoders; open-loop capable Can lose steps under heavy load; not smooth at low speed 3D printers, CNC positioning
Servo Motors Electrical Closed-loop, very precise; high torque-to-size ratio More expensive; needs encoder feedback Industrial robot joints, CNC, aerospace
Hydraulic Fluid pressure Very high force/power density; good for heavy loads Oil leaks, complex plumbing, slow response, noisy Heavy industrial robots, excavators
Pneumatic Compressed air Clean, safe, fast response, low cost Compressible air = less precise position control Pick-and-place, packaging, end-effectors
Analogy: Electric motors = your arm muscles (precise and responsive). Hydraulic = a construction crane (slow but enormous force). Pneumatic = a spring (fast but hard to stop precisely at intermediate positions).

Actuator Control Methods

  • Open-loop (stepper motors): Send pulses to the motor, assume it moves correctly. No position feedback. Works until something slips.
  • Closed-loop (servo motors): Encoder or resolver measures actual position → controller compares to desired → sends corrective signal. PID controller commonly used.

PID Controller (Proportional-Integral-Derivative)

  • P (Proportional): Correction proportional to current error. Faster response but may oscillate.
  • I (Integral): Corrects accumulated past errors. Eliminates steady-state error.
  • D (Derivative): Responds to rate of change of error. Damps oscillations (predictive).
Control Output = Kp×e(t) + Ki×∫e(t)dt + Kd×de(t)/dt Where e(t) = error = desired position − actual position
Analogy: Driving a car to a target speed: P = Press gas based on how far you are from target speed. I = Press more if you've been slow for a long time. D = Ease off gas as you're quickly approaching target (to avoid overshooting).

Linear vs. Non-Linear Control of Manipulators

FeatureLinear ControlNon-Linear Control
AssumesSystem behaves linearly (small motions)Full non-linear robot dynamics
MethodsPID, PD, state feedbackComputed torque, feedback linearization, adaptive control
AccuracyGood for slow, small-range motionBetter for fast, large-range motion
ComplexitySimple to implementComplex, computationally heavy
Real robotsUsed with linearization approximationsNeeded for high-performance robots
Example — Computed Torque Control: This non-linear method computes the exact torques needed (using full dynamic model) to produce desired joint accelerations, then sends those torques to actuators. It accounts for gravity, Coriolis forces, and inertia of all links — something a simple PID ignores.

11. Robot Sensory Devices

Two Basic Categories

  • Internal Sensors: Monitor internal state of robot (joint position, velocity, torque). Used for control loop feedback.
    • Examples: Encoders, resolvers, tachometers, potentiometers
  • External Sensors: Sense the robot's environment and objects. Coordinate robot with surroundings.
    • Examples: Vision cameras, proximity sensors, force/torque sensors, tactile arrays

Types of External Sensors

Sensor TypeSub-typesFunction / Example
Tactile Sensors Touch sensors, force sensors, tactile array sensors Detect contact, measure grasping force. Like fingertip sensitivity in a gripper.
Proximity & Range Sensors Optical (IR/laser), Acoustical (ultrasonic), Electromagnetic Detect objects before touching; measure distance. Used for collision avoidance.
Miscellaneous Sensors Thermocouples, pressure transducers, fluid flow, voice sensors Sense temperature, pressure, flow rate for process monitoring.
Machine Vision Systems Cameras, image processors Object recognition, inspection, position estimation.

Uses of Sensors in Robotics

  • Safety monitoring (stop if human enters workspace)
  • Interlocks in work cell control
  • Part inspection for quality control
  • Determining object position and orientation

Desirable Features of Sensors

  • Accuracy, Operation range, Speed of response
  • Calibration, Reliability, Cost and ease of operation
Analogy: Internal sensors are like your proprioception (sensing your own limb position without looking). External sensors are like your five senses (seeing, touching, hearing what's around you). A robot without external sensors is like working blindfolded.

12. Computer Vision — Imaging Components

What is Computer Vision?

Computer vision is the field that enables machines to interpret and understand visual information from the world (images, video) — essentially giving robots the ability to "see."

Analogy: Just as the human visual system has eyes (image capture), a retina (processing), and the brain (interpretation), a robot vision system has a camera (capture), an image processing unit (pre-processing), and a recognition algorithm (interpretation).

Components of an Imaging System

1. Image Sensing / Capture

  • CCD Camera (Charge-Coupled Device): High-quality image capture, low noise. Used in industrial robots.
  • CMOS Camera: Lower power, faster readout, cheaper. Used in smartphones, modern vision systems.
  • Depth Cameras (RGB-D): Capture color + depth (distance). Example: Microsoft Kinect, Intel RealSense.
  • Stereo Cameras: Two cameras at known separation → triangulate 3D position (like human binocular vision).
  • Structured Light Sensors: Project a known pattern of light → analyze deformation to calculate depth.

2. Image Digitization (Analog to Digital Conversion)

  • Converts analog light signals to digital pixel values
  • Resolution: Number of pixels (e.g., 1920×1080)
  • Bit depth: Levels of intensity per channel (8-bit = 256 levels; 16-bit = 65,536 levels)
  • Frame rate: Images captured per second (fps)

3. Image Pre-Processing

  • Noise Reduction: Gaussian blur, median filter to remove camera noise
  • Edge Detection: Sobel, Canny algorithms to find object boundaries
  • Thresholding: Convert grayscale to binary (object vs. background)
  • Morphological Operations: Erosion and dilation to clean binary images
  • Histogram Equalization: Improve contrast for better feature extraction

4. Feature Extraction

  • Extract meaningful information from pre-processed images
  • Geometric features: Area, perimeter, centroid, bounding box
  • Texture features: Patterns, roughness
  • Keypoint features: SIFT (Scale-Invariant Feature Transform), ORB, SURF
  • These features are passed to the recognition system

5. Image Segmentation

  • Partition image into meaningful regions (separating objects from background)
  • Methods: Thresholding, region growing, watershed algorithm, semantic segmentation (deep learning)
Example Pipeline: A robot on an assembly line needs to identify a defective part. Pipeline: Camera captures image → Gaussian blur (noise removal) → Canny edge detection → Thresholding → Segmentation → Feature extraction (area, aspect ratio) → Classifier (is defective? yes/no) → Robot either picks or discards part.

13. Object Recognition & Categorization

What is Object Recognition?

The process of identifying objects in images — determining what an object is, and often where it is and in what orientation.

Levels of Visual Understanding

  1. Image Classification: "What is in this image?" — Single label for whole image (e.g., "car")
  2. Object Detection: "Where are the objects?" — Bounding boxes + labels (e.g., YOLO, SSD, Faster R-CNN)
  3. Semantic Segmentation: Pixel-level classification (every pixel labeled as "road", "car", "human", etc.)
  4. Instance Segmentation: Like semantic, but distinguishes between different instances of same class
  5. Pose Estimation: Identifies object orientation in 3D space — critical for robot grasping
Analogy: Classification is like asking "Is there a dog in this room?" Detection is like asking "Where exactly is the dog sitting?" Segmentation is like drawing the dog's exact outline pixel by pixel.

Traditional vs. Deep Learning Approaches

FeatureTraditional (Pre-2012)Deep Learning (CNN-based)
FeaturesHand-crafted (SIFT, HOG, SURF)Learned automatically by network
AccuracyLower on complex tasksState-of-the-art accuracy
Data neededWorks with less dataNeeds large labeled datasets
SpeedFaster inferenceSlower (GPU required)
ExamplesTemplate matching, HOG+SVMVGGNet, ResNet, YOLO, Mask R-CNN

Key Deep Learning Architectures for Vision

  • CNN (Convolutional Neural Network): Foundation of modern computer vision. Uses convolutional filters to extract spatial features hierarchically.
  • YOLO (You Only Look Once): Real-time object detection — processes entire image in one pass. Used in industrial robots for fast detection.
  • ResNet: Very deep network with skip connections — avoids vanishing gradient problem.
  • Mask R-CNN: Object detection + instance segmentation. Gives pixel-level mask for each detected object.
Example: A warehouse robot (Amazon Kiva) uses a camera and CNN to identify items on shelves. YOLO detects item positions in real time. The robot grasps items using pose estimation to determine exactly how to orient its gripper.

Need for Vision Training & Adaptation

Why Training is Necessary

  • Real-world conditions vary: different lighting, viewpoints, occlusions, backgrounds
  • Objects look different when partially blocked, rotated, or under different illumination
  • A trained model must generalize beyond its training examples

Vision Training Process

  1. Data Collection: Capture images of objects under various conditions
  2. Annotation/Labeling: Label objects in each image (bounding boxes, segmentation masks)
  3. Training: Feed labeled data into CNN; adjust weights via backpropagation
  4. Validation: Test on unseen images; measure accuracy metrics (precision, recall, mAP)
  5. Fine-tuning: Adjust hyperparameters, add more data, use data augmentation
  6. Deployment: Integrate trained model into robot control system

Adaptation Techniques

  • Transfer Learning: Use a pre-trained model (on ImageNet) and fine-tune on specific robot task. Saves huge training time.
  • Data Augmentation: Artificially expand training data — rotate, flip, crop, add noise to images. Improves robustness.
  • Online Learning / Adaptation: Robot continues learning from new observations during operation. Adapts to changing environments.
  • Domain Adaptation: Train in simulation (cheap), adapt to real world (domain gap reduction).
Analogy for Transfer Learning: A medical student (pre-trained on general biology) specializes in cardiology (fine-tuning on specific domain) rather than starting medical education from scratch. Similarly, a robot vision model pre-trained on millions of images needs only a few thousand labeled robot-specific images to become an expert at the task.

14. Robot Programming

Levels of Robot Programming

  1. Manual Programming (Teach Pendant): Operator physically guides robot to each point; controller records joint positions. Played back during production.
    • Simple, no coding needed; but tedious and requires robot downtime
  2. Textual/Off-line Programming: Write programs in a robot programming language (VAL, RAPID, KRL, etc.) on a computer, then upload.
    • Robot doesn't need to be stopped; program can be tested in simulation first
  3. Task-Level Programming: Specify what to do (e.g., "assemble part A into slot B"), not how — robot figures out the motion. Requires AI/planning.
    • Highest level; still largely in research stage

Key Programming Concepts

  • Waypoints/Points: Stored Cartesian or joint positions the robot must visit
  • Motion Instructions: MoveJ (joint interpolation), MoveL (linear motion), MoveC (circular arc)
  • I/O Control: Set digital outputs (activate gripper, signal conveyor)
  • Conditional Logic: IF sensor = TRUE THEN pick part ELSE reject
  • Loops and Subroutines: Repeat operations, organize code into modular routines

Simulation & Offline Programming Tools

  • ROS (Robot Operating System): Open-source framework for robot software development. Not an OS — middleware that provides communication between robot components.
  • Gazebo: Physics simulation environment for ROS robots
  • RobotStudio (ABB): Official offline programming and simulation tool
  • KUKA.Sim: KUKA's offline simulation environment
Example — Teach Pendant vs. Offline Programming:
Teach pendant: Like teaching a parrot by demonstration — show it the path, it repeats it. Good for simple tasks in a fixed environment.
Offline programming: Like giving someone written directions before they arrive at a new city. More powerful, but requires accurate models of the robot and environment.

15. Robot Design & Process Specifications

Industrial Robot — Key Characteristics

An industrial robot must possess anthropomorphic characteristics — human-like physical structure or ability to respond to sensory signals like humans. Characteristics include:

  • Mechanical arms for various industrial tasks
  • Sensory perceptive devices (sensors) to interact with machines
  • Ability to make simple decisions
  • Multiple moving axes (joints) controlled by a dedicated digital computer

Work Volume / Work Envelope

The 3D space in which a robot can move and operate its wrist end. Also called work space or work envelope.

Factors affecting work volume:

  • Robot anatomy (configuration type)
  • Maximum movement value for each joint
  • Size of robot components (arm, wrist, body)

Robot Design Process (Steps)

  1. Define Task Requirements: What must the robot do? (payload, speed, precision, DOF needed, environment)
  2. Select Configuration: Choose arm type (Cartesian, cylindrical, articulated etc.) based on task and workspace
  3. Select Actuators: Electric, hydraulic, or pneumatic based on force, speed, precision needs
  4. Design Mechanical Structure: Links, joints, materials (weight vs. stiffness trade-off)
  5. Select Sensors: Internal (encoders) and external (vision, force) sensors
  6. Design Control System: Open/closed loop, type of controller (PID, model-based)
  7. Program the Robot: Write task programs, configure I/O, test in simulation
  8. Integration & Testing: Deploy in work cell, validate precision, speed, reliability

Quick Revision — Common Exam Questions

Q: Differentiate between holonomic and non-holonomic constraints with examples.
Holonomic = expressible algebraically (fixed joints, hinges). Non-holonomic = expressed in velocities/accelerations (rolling without slipping, car wheel direction). Holonomic constraints directly reduce DOF; non-holonomic restrict motion path without reducing configuration space DOF.
Q: Explain the difference between accuracy and repeatability in robots.
Accuracy = how close the robot gets to a programmed target (half of resolution interval; affected by mechanical inaccuracies — hard to control, rarely specified by manufacturers). Repeatability = how consistently the robot returns to a previously visited point (repeatability error is random, generally better than accuracy for same robot; larger robots have less precise repeatability).
Q: What is the Lagrangian method and why is it used for robot analysis?
The Lagrangian method derives equations of motion using energy (kinetic − potential) rather than forces. For robots with many links, tracking all forces via Newton's method is complex. Lagrangian elegantly handles this using joint variables as generalized coordinates, yielding the torques needed at each joint to produce desired motion.
Q: Compare fixed, programmable, and flexible automation.
Fixed (hard): Special-purpose, fixed sequence, low cost/unit, high output, inflexible. Programmable (soft): Program-controlled, changeable sequence, flexible for product variations, batch production suitable. Flexible: Extension of programmable — no downtime for changeover, continuous mix of products, highest investment.
Q: What is transfer learning and why is it important in robot vision?
Transfer learning reuses a model pre-trained on large datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and fine-tunes it on a smaller, task-specific dataset. Saves massive computation time and data. Critical in robotics where labeled training data is expensive to collect. A robot vision system can leverage millions of pre-learned visual features (edges, shapes, textures) and specialize with just hundreds of robot-specific images.
Q: What are the advantages and disadvantages of the jointed arm (RRR) configuration?
Advantages: Maximum flexibility, huge spherical work volume, quick operation, most dexterous. Disadvantages: Very expensive, difficult operating procedures, many mechanical components, complex kinematics calculations. Applications: Arc welding, spray coating. Example robot: SCARA.

Advantages & Disadvantages of Robotic Systems

Advantages

  • Safety in hazardous environments
  • Speed and consistency
  • High accuracy and repeatability
  • Multi-skill capability
  • Increased productivity and efficiency
  • Reduces employee workload
  • Creates new industries and jobs
  • Technology spillover to other fields

Disadvantages

  • Less dexterous than humans
  • Increased military use concerns
  • High economic cost to companies
  • Needs skilled operators and programmers
  • Lacks human intelligence and judgment
  • Job loss and unemployment
  • AI capability concerns
